Selected Process and Definition

This page focuses on erosion by running water and how moving water steadily changes landforms over time.

Selected Surface Process: Erosion by running water, also called stream erosion.

Scientific definition: Erosion is the physical process in which weathered material is removed from one place and transported to another location. In this case, running water acts as a major erosional force because it carries surface runoff downslope, moves sediment through channels, and transfers material into larger basins.

That repeated movement does more than reshape the stream itself. Over time, it lowers valleys, widens banks, and sets the stage for features such as meanders, gorges, and floodplains.

Geological Mechanisms

These three processes explain how streams change the shape of the land.

A

Stream Channel Development

When there is enough water supply, surface water begins to move downslope in broad, undefined paths. As the flow repeats over time, the water cuts a permanent path into sediment called a stream channel. If the water stays in a confined space, it can erode deeper and wider, building distinct stream banks along the sides.

B

Carrying Capacity and Discharge

A stream’s carrying capacity is its ability to transport material, and it depends on velocity and total water volume. Discharge is the amount of water flowing past a point in a given time, so a larger discharge usually means the stream can carry more sediment and larger particles.

C

Meander Formation

Water does not travel at the same speed everywhere in a channel. The center of the stream usually moves fastest, while friction slows the edges. That difference in speed bends the channel into a meander, and the faster water on the outside of the curve erodes the bank more strongly.

How discharge changes stream power

When a stream carries more water, it can move more sediment and reshape its channel more quickly. That effect is strongest during storms and other high-flow events.

Discharge = width × depth × velocity (m3/s)

As discharge increases, carrying capacity grows too, allowing streams to move suspended particles and push heavier material along the bottom as bed load. During storms, this change can happen quickly and reshape the channel more aggressively. That is why high-flow events often leave the clearest erosion marks.

3

Real-World Case Study: Canyons and Gorges

A river can build dramatic landscapes when erosion continues for a long time.

As streams erode downward through sediment or solid bedrock over long periods, a steep, V-shaped channel forms. That continuous cutting creates landforms such as canyons and gorges, especially where the water has strong flow and a steep slope.

The Niagara Gorge: The Niagara Gorge formed as the Niagara River rushed over the falls and eroded the rock below. As the waterfall retreated upstream, it left behind a deep, steep-walled gorge that still shows the power of moving water.

Deposition dynamic: When a stream loses velocity, it also loses carrying capacity. Sediment then drops out of the water and collects in fan-shaped deposits where the river reaches flatter ground, often near the mouth of a stream or where the slope becomes gentler.

4

Application and Hazards Analysis

Downstream flooding is one of the clearest hazards connected to stream erosion, heavy runoff, and sudden changes in river flow.

The hazard: When heavy precipitation falls, or when soil has low porosity and cannot absorb water well, excess runoff enters river systems quickly. If the water rises above the stream banks and spreads onto nearby land, it creates a flood. When that happens farther downstream, the result can be a large downstream flood event that affects entire communities.

Environmental impact: Floodwater carries large amounts of eroded sediment away from its original location. That movement strips away fertile topsoil, changes soil porosity, and alters the shape of river basins, wetlands, and nearby ecosystems. It can also leave behind new deposits in low-lying areas.

Human impact: Downstream floods can cause expensive damage to communities. Floodwaters destroy infrastructure, weaken building foundations, flood roads, and create long-term safety and financial problems for residents. In severe cases, cleanup and recovery can take months or longer.

Modern management: Environmental scientists use tracking technology, satellites, and gauging stations to collect real-time stream data and monitor changing conditions. That information helps communities issue warnings, prepare evacuation plans, and respond sooner.
