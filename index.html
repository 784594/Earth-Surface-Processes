Selected Process and Definition

This page focuses on erosion by running water and how moving water steadily changes landforms over time.

Selected Surface Process: Erosion by running water, also called stream erosion.

Scientific definition: Erosion is the process in which weathered material is removed from one place and transported to another. In this case, running water acts as a major erosional force because it moves surface runoff downslope, carries sediment through channels, and transfers material into larger basins.

Stream erosion is an important part of Earth's surface systems because moving water transfers sediment between different environments. This process helps shape landscapes, but when erosion becomes too intense it can create hazards such as flooding and land degradation.

Over time, that movement lowers valleys, widens stream banks, and helps form features such as meanders, gorges, and floodplains.

2

Geological Mechanisms

These three processes explain how streams change the shape of the land.

A

Stream Channel Development

When a stream has enough water, surface flow begins to move downslope in broad, undefined paths. As the flow repeats over time, it cuts a permanent route into sediment called a channel. If the water stays confined, it can erode deeper and wider and build distinct banks along the sides.

B

Carrying Capacity and Discharge

A stream's carrying capacity is its ability to transport material, and it depends on velocity and total water volume. Discharge is the amount of water flowing past a point in a given time, so a larger discharge usually means the stream can carry more sediment and larger particles.

C

Meander Formation

Water does not travel at the same speed everywhere in a channel. The center of the stream usually moves fastest, while friction slows the edges. That difference in speed bends the channel into a meander, and the faster water on the outside of the curve erodes the bank more strongly.

How discharge changes stream power

When a stream carries more water, it can move more sediment and reshape its channel more quickly. That effect is strongest during storms and other high-flow events.

Discharge = width x depth x velocity (m3/s)

As discharge increases, streams can carry more sediment. Smaller particles can stay mixed within the flowing water, while larger rocks and gravel are pushed or rolled along the stream bed.

Faster flow also increases the stream's energy, which leads to more abrasion against the channel floor and banks, more undercutting on outer bends, and more transport of sand, gravel, and silt. When the water slows, the same sediment is deposited again, so erosion and deposition work together to keep changing the landscape.

3

Real-World Case Study: Canyons and Gorges

A river can build dramatic landscapes when erosion continues for a long time.

First example · Ontario, Canada

The Niagara Gorge and Niagara River

As streams erode downward through sediment or solid bedrock over long periods, a steep, V-shaped channel forms. This continuous cutting creates landforms such as canyons and gorges, especially where the water has a strong flow and a steep slope.

The Niagara Gorge formed as the Niagara River flowed over the falls and eroded the rock below. As the waterfall retreated upstream, it left behind a deep, steep-walled gorge that shows the power of moving water.

At Niagara Parks' White Water Walk, the Whirlpool Rapids flow at about 48 km/h, showing the high-energy water movement that continues through the Niagara Gorge.

When a stream loses velocity, it also loses carrying capacity. Sediment then drops out of the water and collects in fan-shaped deposits where the river reaches flatter ground, often near the mouth of a stream or where the slope becomes gentler.

Second example · Arizona, United States

The Grand Canyon and the Colorado River

The Grand Canyon shows how a river can carve a landscape over millions of years. The Colorado River has cut down through the uplifted Colorado Plateau, exposing a long sequence of sedimentary rock layers.

Its steep gradient, fast-moving water, and heavy sediment load allow the Colorado River to erode the landscape over long periods of time.

This example makes an important comparison with Niagara Gorge: both landforms reveal stream erosion, but their scale, rock layers, climate, and river systems are different. In both places, flowing water transports sediment and changes the landscape.

Application and Hazards Analysis

Downstream flooding is one of the clearest hazards connected to stream erosion, heavy runoff, and sudden changes in river flow.

The hazard: When heavy precipitation falls, or when soil has low porosity and cannot absorb water well, excess runoff enters river systems quickly. If the water rises above the stream banks and spreads onto nearby land, it creates a flood. When that happens farther downstream, the result can be a downstream flood that affects entire communities.

Environmental impact: Floodwater carries large amounts of eroded sediment away from its original location. That movement strips away fertile topsoil, changes soil porosity, and alters the shape of river basins, wetlands, and nearby ecosystems. It can also leave behind new deposits in low-lying areas.

Human impact: Downstream floods can cause expensive damage to communities. Floodwaters destroy infrastructure, weaken building foundations, flood roads, and create long-term safety and financial problems for residents. In severe cases, cleanup and recovery can take months or longer.

Management and solutions: Restoring vegetation along riverbanks can help hold soil in place and slow runoff. Floodplain management and avoiding construction in high-risk flood areas can also reduce damage when rivers overflow.

Modern management: Environmental scientists use tracking technology, satellites, and gauging stations to collect real-time stream data and monitor changing conditions. That information helps communities issue warnings, prepare evacuation plans, and respond sooner.

Regional analysis: In the Niagara River region, strong flow and a steep drop create conditions where erosion acts quickly. Geologically, the river continues cutting into bedrock and widening the gorge over time. Environmentally, the moving water transports sediment and changes habitats along the river corridor. For people and communities, tourism, infrastructure, and nearby neighborhoods depend on careful monitoring because faster erosion or high water can damage trails, roads, lookout areas, and riverfront development.

Future impacts: Climate change is expected to increase the frequency of extreme rainfall in many regions, which could lead to greater runoff, faster erosion, and more frequent flooding. More intense rainfall can increase runoff, flooding, erosion, and sediment movement, making river management and flood preparedness even more important.
